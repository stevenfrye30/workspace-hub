Sun & Moon Letters — The Definite Article ال
▼

ال (al-) is the definite article. Before Moon letters the lām is pronounced. Before Sun letters the lām assimilates — it is silently replaced by the following letter (written doubled with a shadda).

The Hamzah ء
▼

Hamzah represents the glottal stop /ʔ/ — the catch in the throat between syllables (as in English "uh-oh"). Its written form depends on the surrounding vowel environment.

SeatFormContextExample
Alif seat (above) أ initial, preceded by /a/ or /u/ أَكَلَ

Tāʼ Marbūṭah ة
▼

Tāʼ marbūṭah (lit. "tied tāʼ") marks feminine gender. It looks like a Hāʼ with two dots above. Its pronunciation depends on context:

In isolation / pause: pronounced /a/ — مَدِينَة madīna (city)
In construct state (iḍāfa) or before a vowel: pronounced /at/ — مَدِينَةُ القَاهِرَة madīnatu l-Qāhira (the city of Cairo)
Gender function: adds feminine gender to an otherwise masculine noun — مُعَلِّم (male teacher) → مُعَلِّمَة (female teacher)
The Arabic Writing System

Arabic is an abjad — a consonant alphabet in which vowels are generally not written. It has 28 letters. Text is written right-to-left, though numerals run left-to-right within a line.

Most letters connect to the following letter within a word. Six letters (Alif, Dāl, Dhāl, Rāʼ, Zāy, Wāw) only connect on the right — they never connect to the letter that follows them.

Vowel diacritics (ḥarakāt) exist but are mostly omitted in everyday text, newspapers, and books. They appear in the Quran, children's materials, and learning resources.

Gender الجنس

All Arabic nouns are either masculine or feminine. Feminine nouns typically (not always) end in ة (tāʼ marbūṭah). Some nouns are inherently feminine regardless of ending: paired body parts (يَد yad — hand), countries, cities.

Adjectives must agree in gender with the noun they modify:

بَيْت كَبِير
bayt kabīr
a big house (masc.)
مَدِينَة كَبِيرَة
madīna kabīra
a big city (fem.)
The Definite Article ال

ال (al-) is prefixed directly to the noun and never stands alone. It does not change for gender or number. With sun letters, the lām assimilates to the following consonant (shown with a shadda). The hamzah in ال (hamzat al-waṣl) is elided when a vowel precedes it in connected speech.

بَيْت ← البَيْت
bayt → al-bayt
a house → the house (moon letter)
شَمْس ← الشَّمْس
shams → ash-shams
a sun → the sun (sun letter — lām assimilates)

Verb Conjugation — Past Tense الماضي

Arabic verbs are built on 3-letter roots. The root ك-ت-ب (k-t-b) relates to writing. Past tense is formed by vowel patterns applied to the root.

ArabicRomanizationPerson
كَتَبَ kataba he wrote
كَتَبَتْ katabat she wrote
كَتَبْتَ katabta you (m.) wrote
كَتَبْتِ katabti you (f.) wrote
كَتَبْتُ katabtu I wrote
كَتَبُوا katabū they (m.) wrote
كَتَبْنَ katabna they (f.) wrote
كَتَبْنَا katabnā we wrote
Present Tense المضارع

The present/imperfect tense uses prefixes and suffixes applied to the root. The prefix indicates person; the suffix indicates gender and number.

ArabicRomanizationPerson
يَكْتُبُ yaktubu he writes
تَكْتُبُ taktubu she writes / you (m.) write
أَكْتُبُ aktubu I write
نَكْتُبُ naktubu we write
يَكْتُبُونَ yaktubūna they (m.) write

Sentence Structure

Classical Arabic uses Verb-Subject-Object (VSO) order. Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) and spoken dialects often use Subject-Verb-Object (SVO).

ذَهَبَ الوَلَدُ إِلَى المَدْرَسَة
dhahaba l-waladu ilā l-madrasa
"The boy went to school" — VSO (classical)
الوَلَدُ ذَهَبَ إِلَى المَدْرَسَة
al-waladu dhahaba ilā l-madrasa
"The boy went to school" — SVO (modern)
Noun Phrases

Adjectives follow the noun and agree in gender, number, and definiteness. When the noun is definite (has ال), the adjective must also take ال.

بَيْت كَبِير
bayt kabīr
a big house (indefinite)
البَيْتُ الكَبِير
al-baytu l-kabīr
the big house (definite — adjective also takes ال)
Construct State الإضافة — Iḍāfa

Possession is expressed by juxtaposing two nouns. The first noun (possessed item) takes no ال and no tanwīn. The second noun (possessor) may be definite.

كِتَابُ الطَّالِب
kitābu l-ṭālib
the student's book (lit. "book-of the-student")
بَابُ البَيْت
bābu l-bayt
the door of the house

The Ten Arabic Verb Forms الأوزانُ العَشَرَة

Arabic verbs derive from trilateral (3-letter) roots through 10 standard patterns (وَزن wazn). Each form adds a predictable meaning to the base root — learning the patterns unlocks thousands of words. Forms shown using ك-ت-ب (k-t-b, “write”) and other common roots.

I
فَعَلَ / يَفْعُلُ fa‘ala / yaf‘ulu Basic action — to [verb]

The root form. No added consonants. Vowel pattern varies (fa‘ala / fa‘ila / fa‘ula) and must be memorized per root. Most common and productive form in Arabic.

Perfect (he wrote)كَتَبَkataba
Imperfect (he writes)يَكتُبُyaktubu
Active participleكَاتِبkātib — “writer”
Verbal noun (maṣdar)كِتَابَةkitāba — “writing”
II
فَعَّلَ / يُفَعِّلُ fa‘‘ala / yufa‘‘ilu Causative / Intensive / Denominative

Middle consonant doubled (shadda). Often causative (“make someone do”) or intensifies Form I. Also forms verbs from nouns and adjectives.

Perfect (he smashed)كَسَّرَkassara
Imperfectيُكَسِّرُyukassiru
Active participleمُكَسِّرmukassir
Verbal nounتَكْسِيرtaksīr
III
فَاعَلَ / يُفَاعِلُ fā‘ala / yufā‘ilu Mutual / Reciprocal action with another

Long ā inserted after first radical. Implies action directed at or shared with another person. كَتَبَ “wrote” → كَاتَبَ “corresponded with.”

Perfect (corresponded)كَاتَبَkātaba
Imperfectيُكَاتِبُyukātibu
Active participleمُكَاتِبmukātib
Verbal nounمُكَاتَبَةmukātaba
IV
أَفْعَلَ / يُفْعِلُ af‘ala / yuf‘ilu Causative — “make X happen”

Prefixed أَ in perfect; yufʿilu in imperfect. Similar to Form II but often for single, completed causation. Very common in MSA formal writing.

Perfect (he informed)أَخْبَرَakhbara
Imperfectيُخْبِرُyukhbiru
Active participleمُخْبِرmukhbir — “informer”
Verbal nounإِخْبَارiẖbār
V
تَفَعَّلَ / يَتَفَعَّلُ tafa‘‘ala / yatafa‘‘alu Reflexive of Form II

تَ prefix on Form II. Action happens to the subject (reflexive) or is intransitive. عَلَّمَ “taught” → تَعَلَّمَ “learned (by oneself).” Extremely productive form.

Perfect (he learned)تَعَلَّمَta‘allama
Imperfectيَتَعَلَّمُyata‘allamu
Active participleمُتَعَلِّمmuta‘allim — “student”
Verbal nounتَعَلُّمta‘allum
VI
تَفَاعَلَ / يَتَفَاعَلُ tafā‘ala / yatafā‘alu Reflexive / Reciprocal of Form III

تَ prefix on Form III. Two or more subjects perform the action mutually to each other, or the action is simulated/pretended. تَكَاتَبَ “they corresponded with each other.”

Perfect (they spoke)تَحَدَّثَtaḥaddatha
Imperfectيَتَحَدَّثُyataḥaddathu
Active participleمُتَحَدِّثmutaḥaddith — “speaker”
Verbal nounتَحَدُّثtaḥadduth
VII
اِنْفَعَلَ / يَنْفَعِلُ infa‘ala / yanfa‘ilu Passive / Intransitive of Form I

اِنْ prefix. The subject undergoes the action spontaneously rather than performing it. Not formed from roots beginning with ر, ل, م, ن, و, or ي.

Perfect (it broke)اِنْكَسَرَinkasara
Imperfectيَنْكَسِرُyankasiru
Active participleمُنْكَسِرmunkasir — “broken”
Verbal nounاِنْكِسَارinkisār
VIII
اِفْتَعَلَ / يَفْتَعِلُ ifta‘ala / yafta‘ilu Reflexive of Form I / Middle voice

اِ prefix + ت infixed after first radical. Often “for oneself” or collective action. Note: emphatic roots (ص ض ط ظ) cause the ت to assimilate (ت+ص → طط, etc.).

Perfect (he participated)اِشْتَرَكَishtaraka
Imperfectيَشْتَرِكُyashtariku
Active participleمُشْتَرِكmushtarik — “participant”
Verbal nounاِشْتِرَاكshtirāk
IX
اِفْعَلَّ / يَفْعَلُّ if‘alla / yaf‘allu Colors & Physical states only

Rare; final radical doubled. Used exclusively for colors and physical/bodily states. Root ح-م-ر (red) → اِحْمَرَّ “turned/became red.”

Perfect (turned red)اِحْمَرَّiḥmarra
Imperfectيَحْمَرُّyaḥmarru
Active participleمُحْمَرّmuḥmarr — “reddened”
Verbal nounاِحْمِرَارiḥmirār
X
اِسْتَفْعَلَ / يَسْتَفْعِلُ istaf‘ala / yastaf‘ilu Requestive / Considerative

اِسْتَ prefix. “To deem X as…” or “to request X.” Highly productive in MSA for coining new vocabulary. اِسْتَعْمَلَ “to use / employ (deem fit for use).”

Perfect (he used)اِسْتَعْمَلَista‘mala
Imperfectيَسْتَعْمِلُyasta‘milu
Active participleمُسْتَعْمِلmusta‘mil — “user”
Verbal nounاِسْتِعْمَالisti‘māl — “usage”
