Africa

Bronze, stone, cloth, and mud — craft from across the continent.
← All regions

Africa's art tradition is vast and old. These pieces range from 9th-century Igbo bronzes to the largest mud-brick building in the world.

Bronze Head from Ife
Yoruba · 12th–15th c. · Nigeria
Naturalistic portraits cast in brass with lost-wax technique. When first seen in Europe they were disbelieved as African work.
Benin Bronzes
Edo kingdom · 16th–17th c. · Nigeria
Plaques that once covered pillars of the royal palace in Benin City. Looted in 1897; still being repatriated today.
Bronze Head of Queen Idia
Benin kingdom · 16th c. · Nigeria
Portrait of the first Queen Mother of Benin, cast to sit on an ancestral altar. One of the most famous of the Benin bronzes.
Igbo-Ukwu Bronze
Igbo · 9th c. · Nigeria
Lost-wax castings of startling delicacy — three centuries before Ife, and predating most European bronzework of the same technical sophistication.
Great Zimbabwe
Shona · 11th–15th c. · Zimbabwe
Dry-stone walls built without mortar, enclosing a trade capital that controlled the gold road to the Indian Ocean.
Great Mosque of Kilwa
Swahili coast · 11th–14th c. · Tanzania
Coral-stone mosque on an island that dominated Indian Ocean trade. Ibn Battuta called Kilwa "one of the most beautiful cities in the world."
Rock-Hewn Churches of Lalibela
Zagwe dynasty · 12th–13th c. · Ethiopia
Eleven churches carved downward into solid rock. Meant as a new Jerusalem after pilgrimage routes were cut off.
Great Mosque of Djenné
Mali · rebuilt 1907 (earlier mosque 13th c.)
The world's largest mud-brick building. Every spring the whole town replasters it by hand in a single day.
Dogon Cliff Villages
Dogon · 14th c. onward · Mali
Mud granaries and shrines stacked along the Bandiagara Escarpment. The Dogon moved here fleeing forced conversion and stayed half a millennium.
Kente Cloth
Asante & Ewe · 17th c. onward · Ghana
Hand-woven silk and cotton strips sewn together into royal cloth. Each pattern has a name and a proverb behind it.
