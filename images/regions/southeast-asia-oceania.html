Southeast Asia & Oceania

Temple cities, island stone, and the deepest-time painting tradition on earth.
← All regions

From the sandstone temple-mountains of Angkor and Borobudur to basalt moai on Easter Island and rock shelters in northern Australia where painting has gone on for 20,000 years.

Angkor Wat
Khmer Empire · 12th c. · Cambodia
The largest religious monument on earth. Built as a Hindu temple to Vishnu, converted to Buddhist use, and never fully abandoned.
Borobudur
Sailendra dynasty · 9th c. · Java, Indonesia
A stone mandala you walk. Climbing its nine terraces takes you through 2,672 relief panels and 504 Buddhas to the empty stupa at the top.
Bagan
Pagan Kingdom · 11th–13th c. · Myanmar
More than 2,000 Buddhist temples and stupas on a plain by the Irrawaddy. At dawn hot air balloons drift over a landscape of brick towers.
Moai
Rapa Nui · 1250–1500 · Easter Island
Nearly 900 stone figures, carved and moved miles from the quarry. They face inland — watching over the villages, not out to sea.
Ubirr Rock Art
Aboriginal Australian · up to 20,000 years old · Kakadu
One of the oldest continuous art traditions on earth. Later paintings show fish anatomy so precisely that species are still identifiable.
Māori Wharenui
Aotearoa · 18th c. onward · New Zealand
A carved meeting house represents an ancestor's body — ridgepole as spine, rafters as ribs. Every panel tells a story in whakairo carving.
