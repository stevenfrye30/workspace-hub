South Asia

Stupas, cave temples, bronze gods, and marble mausoleums.
← All regions

Hindu, Buddhist, Jain, and Islamic traditions layered on top of each other for two millennia. Much of the greatest work is architecture carved straight out of living rock.

Taj Mahal
Mughal · 1632–1653 · Agra, India
A tomb Shah Jahan built for his wife Mumtaz Mahal, who died in childbirth. The marble is inlaid with 28 kinds of gem and stone.
Ajanta Caves
Buddhist · 2nd c. BCE – 6th c. CE · Maharashtra
Thirty rock-cut monasteries with surviving murals of the Buddha's past lives. Abandoned, forgotten, and rediscovered by a tiger-hunting British officer in 1819.
Konark Sun Temple
Eastern Ganga dynasty · 13th c. · Odisha
A massive stone chariot for the sun god, pulled by seven horses, wheels carved to tell the time from their shadows.
Khajuraho Temples
Chandela dynasty · 950–1050 · Madhya Pradesh
Hindu and Jain temples covered in dense sculpture — gods, battles, and the frank erotic scenes the site is best known for.
Nataraja (Shiva as Lord of Dance)
Chola dynasty · 10th–11th c. · Tamil Nadu
Shiva dances the universe into being and out of it again, surrounded by a ring of flame, trampling the demon of ignorance.
Great Stupa at Sanchi
Mauryan → Shunga · 3rd c. BCE onward
A hemispherical mound containing relics of the Buddha. The four carved gateways around it narrate his life without ever depicting him directly.
