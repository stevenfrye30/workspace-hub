Americas

Pre-Columbian builders, painters, and potters — Mexico to the Andes.
← All regions

Cities larger than any in contemporary Europe, pyramid complexes aligned to the stars, and some of the finest portrait pottery ever made — all built without iron, wheel, or draft animals.

Olmec Colossal Heads
Olmec · 1200–400 BCE · Gulf coast, Mexico
Seventeen basalt heads, up to ten feet tall, each with a distinct face. Carved with stone tools and floated on rafts from the quarry, 60 miles away.
Teotihuacan
Pre-Aztec · 100 BCE – 550 CE · Mexico
A city of over 100,000 at its peak — larger than Rome at the same time. Already abandoned when the Aztecs found it and named it "the place where gods were born."
Tikal
Classic Maya · 400 BCE – 900 CE · Guatemala
Step pyramids rising above the rainforest canopy. A Maya superpower that swallowed rival cities until a long drought took it down.
Palenque
Classic Maya · 226 BCE – 799 CE · Chiapas
A small Maya kingdom with exquisite stucco portraits and one of the most carefully decoded royal tombs in the Americas — that of Pakal the Great.
Moche Culture
Moche · 100–700 CE · Northern Peru
Before the Inca, the Moche built painted pyramids and made portrait pots so individual you can tell one person from another across fifteen hundred years.
Nazca Lines
Nazca culture · 500 BCE – 500 CE · Peru
Hummingbirds, monkeys, and spiders drawn at the scale of football fields by clearing pebbles from the desert floor. Visible only from the air.
Chichen Itza
Post-Classic Maya · 600–1200 CE · Yucatán
On the spring equinox, sunlight strikes the pyramid's stairway to form a serpent that seems to slither down the temple of Kukulkan.
Coatlicue
Mexica · late 15th c. · Tenochtitlan
"She of the Serpent Skirt" — the earth goddess, severed neck bleeding two snakes, wearing a necklace of hands and hearts. Found under Mexico City in 1790.
Aztec Sun Stone
Mexica · late 15th c. · Tenochtitlan
A basalt disk over three meters across, carving the five successive suns of Aztec cosmology. Buried under the Zócalo in Mexico City until 1790.
Sacsayhuamán
Inca · 15th c. · Cusco, Peru
A fortress-temple above Cusco built from stones weighing up to 200 tons, fitted so precisely a sheet of paper won't pass between them.
Machu Picchu
Inca · 15th c. · Peru
A royal estate on a knife-edged ridge. The stones of its walls are cut so precisely that no mortar holds them — and they have survived five centuries of earthquakes.
