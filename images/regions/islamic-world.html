Islamic World

From Al-Andalus to Isfahan — geometry, calligraphy, tile, and dome.
← All regions

Islamic art spans three continents and fourteen centuries. Without figural imagery at its centre, it found other languages: endless pattern, abstract color, script as image, and vast cool interiors that turn geometry into prayer.

Alhambra
Nasrid dynasty · 13th–14th c. · Granada, Spain
A palace-citadel of courtyards, fountains, and muqarnas ceilings. The poems inscribed on the walls are part of the architecture.
Dome of the Rock
Umayyad · 691 CE · Jerusalem
The earliest surviving major Islamic building. The gold dome marks the rock from which Muhammad is said to have ascended on the Night Journey.
Mezquita of Córdoba
Umayyad of al-Andalus · 784 CE onward
A forest of red-and-white striped arches from a great caliphate. A cathedral was dropped into the middle of it in the 16th century.
Shah Mosque, Isfahan
Safavid Persia · 1611–1629 · Iran
Seven-colour tilework on the scale of a whole square. Inside the dome, one clap echoes seven times.
Blue Mosque (Sultan Ahmed)
Ottoman · 1609–1616 · Istanbul
The Ottoman answer to Hagia Sophia across the square. More than 20,000 İznik tiles line the interior — the blue that gave it its Western name.
İznik Pottery
Ottoman · 15th–17th c. · Anatolia
The workshop town that supplied tiles for mosques from Istanbul to Damascus. The signature tomato-red slip was a short-lived chemical triumph.
