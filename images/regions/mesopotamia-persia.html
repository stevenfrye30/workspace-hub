Mesopotamia & Persia

Cradles of civilization — Sumer, Assyria, Babylon, and the Achaemenids.
← All regions

Between the Tigris and Euphrates, writing, law codes, and the first cities. Further east, the Persian empire spread a visual language of winged kings and processional staircases from Egypt to the Indus.

Ishtar Gate
Neo-Babylonian · c. 575 BCE
Glazed brick gate to Babylon under Nebuchadnezzar II. Dragons and bulls in cobalt blue. Reconstructed in Berlin's Pergamon Museum.
Code of Hammurabi
Old Babylonian · c. 1754 BCE
Diorite stele, seven feet tall. 282 laws in cuneiform — "an eye for an eye" comes from here. Now in the Louvre.
Standard of Ur
Sumerian · c. 2600 BCE
A wooden box inlaid with lapis, shell, and red limestone. One side shows war; the other, a banquet after the victory.
Lamassu
Neo-Assyrian · 9th–7th c. BCE
Winged bulls with human heads, guarding palace gates at Nineveh and Nimrud. Carved with five legs so they look correct from front and side.
Persepolis
Achaemenid Persia · 518 BCE onward · Iran
Ceremonial capital of Darius and Xerxes. Reliefs show envoys from every corner of the empire bringing tribute. Burned by Alexander in 330 BCE.
Cyrus Cylinder
Achaemenid Persia · c. 539 BCE
A clay cylinder inscribed after Cyrus took Babylon. Often called the first charter of human rights — a claim the British Museum treats with more caution.
