East Asia

China, Japan, Korea — from terracotta armies to woodblock waves.
← All regions

East Asian art has a long memory. Forms travel — from Chinese brushwork to Japanese ukiyo-e, from Tang Buddhism to Korean celadon — and each reinvention carries the earlier one inside it.

Terracotta Army
Qin dynasty · 210 BCE · Xi'an, China
Over 8,000 soldiers, each with a different face, buried with the first emperor of China to guard him after death. Farmers digging a well found them in 1974.
Great Wall of China
Ming (current form) · 14th–17th c.
Not one wall but many, rebuilt across dynasties. The Ming sections visitors see today are a fraction of a system that once stretched over 13,000 miles.
Forbidden City
Ming & Qing · 1406–1420 onward · Beijing
980 buildings on 180 acres. For five centuries, commoners who entered uninvited were executed. It is now the world's most-visited museum.
Along the River During Qingming
Northern Song · Zhang Zeduan · 12th c.
A five-meter handscroll showing hundreds of people in the Song capital on a spring festival day. Read right to left — it unfolds as you walk past.
The Great Wave off Kanagawa
Edo Japan · Hokusai · c. 1831
A woodblock print sold for the price of a bowl of noodles. Mount Fuji sits small and steady under the claw of the wave. Hokusai was in his seventies.
Hōryū-ji
Asuka period · c. 607 CE · Nara, Japan
Among the oldest wooden buildings still standing anywhere in the world. Built when Buddhism had only recently reached Japan.
