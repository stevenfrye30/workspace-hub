Mediterranean

Greek and Roman antiquity — the classical forms that Europe keeps returning to.
← All regions

The classical world's obsession with the human body, proportion, and idealized form shaped every European revival that came after — Renaissance, Neoclassical, and on. Much of it survives only in fragments.

Palace of Knossos
Minoan · c. 1900 BCE · Crete
A sprawling Bronze Age palace with running water, frescoes of dolphins and bull-leapers, and a storage maze that may be the origin of the Minotaur myth.
The Parthenon
Classical Greek · 447–432 BCE · Athens
Temple to Athena on the Acropolis. The columns lean inward and swell slightly in the middle — optical corrections so they look perfectly straight.
Charioteer of Delphi
Early Classical · c. 478 BCE
One of the few surviving Greek bronzes. His calm expression, inset with glass and copper eyes, is a moment in a victory lap — all force held still.
Discobolus
Classical Greek · c. 450 BCE (Roman copy)
Myron's discus thrower — the original bronze is lost; only Roman marble copies survive. A moment just before release, coiled and still.
Venus de Milo
Hellenistic Greek · c. 130–100 BCE
Marble Aphrodite found on the island of Milos in 1820. The missing arms are part of her fame — we don't know what she was doing.
Winged Victory of Samothrace
Hellenistic · c. 200 BCE
Nike landing on the prow of a ship, wind pressing her drapery against her body. One of the most physically dynamic pieces of ancient sculpture.
Laocoön and His Sons
Hellenistic · 1st c. BCE – 1st c. CE
A Trojan priest and his sons strangled by sea serpents. Dug up in a vineyard in 1506 and hauled to the Vatican. Michelangelo studied it for weeks.
Augustus of Prima Porta
Early Roman Empire · c. 15 CE
The emperor posed as a general, barefoot like a god. The breastplate shows the return of captured Roman standards — a diplomatic win dressed as conquest.
The Colosseum
Flavian · 70–80 CE · Rome
An elliptical amphitheater for 50,000 spectators. The underground hypogeum ran elevators, flooded the floor for sea battles, and housed wild animals.
Trajan's Column
Imperial Roman · 113 CE
A 100-foot marble column whose spiral frieze narrates the Dacian Wars in 155 continuous scenes — history carved as a comic strip.
Pompeii
Roman · buried 79 CE
Vesuvius sealed a whole Roman town in volcanic ash. Frescoes, bread loaves, graffiti, and the shapes of bodies all survived — daily life frozen.
