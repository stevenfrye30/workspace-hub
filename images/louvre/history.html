1190 – today

The building is older than almost anything inside it.

1190 — A Fortress on the Seine

Philip II Augustus · Medieval Louvre

Built by King Philip II as he prepared to leave on the Third Crusade, the original Louvre was a fortress with a circular keep (the Grosse Tour) at the western edge of Paris. Its moat walls survive in the basement — you can walk alongside them today, in the cool stone corridors beneath the museum.

1546 — A Renaissance Palace

Francis I · Pierre Lescot

Francis I — the king who brought Leonardo to France and bought the Mona Lisa — ordered the medieval fortress demolished. His architect Pierre Lescot built a Renaissance palace in its place. The elegant western wing of the Cour Carrée is Lescot's work and still stands.

1793 — The Museum Opens

The French Revolution

Ten months after the execution of Louis XVI, the National Assembly declared the royal collection the property of the people. The Louvre opened as a public museum on August 10, 1793. Entrance was free. It has never closed again for more than a few days at a time — except during the two World Wars.

1989 — The Pyramid

I. M. Pei

President Mitterrand commissioned the Chinese-American architect I. M. Pei to redesign the entrance. His glass pyramid — 21 meters tall, held together by thin steel — was almost universally hated when it was announced. It is now as beloved as the palace around it.

Today

~9 million visitors a year

The most visited museum in the world. Over 35,000 works on display, drawn from a collection of more than 500,000. To see every work for thirty seconds would take about four months of continuous walking, without sleep.
