4000 BCE – 4th century CE · Sully Wing

Founded in 1826 with Champollion himself as its first curator — the man who cracked the Rosetta Stone.

The Seated Scribe

c. 2600–2350 BCE · Painted limestone, inlaid eyes · Sully Wing

Roughly 4,500 years old. His eyes — rock crystal set in copper — are what stop you: unnervingly alive, tracking you as you move. His name and title are lost. He holds a papyrus roll, alert, waiting for dictation to begin.

A quiet detail: scribes were usually shown as lean and attentive. This one has the soft belly of a prosperous life — a subtle boast by whoever commissioned him.

Great Sphinx of Tanis

c. 2600 BCE · Pink granite · Sully Wing

Discovered in 1825 in the ruins of the temple of Amun at Tanis. Over 4,500 years old and carved from a single block of granite. The names of at least four pharaohs — each claiming it for themselves — are carved on it.

Reliefs from the Tomb of Seti I

c. 1290 BCE · Painted limestone · Sully Wing

The goddess Hathor welcomes Seti I into the afterlife. Cut from the walls of KV17 in the Valley of the Kings in the 19th century — a practice we now consider vandalism, but which saved this relief from the flooding that ravaged the tomb.

Stele of King Djet (The Serpent King)

c. 3000 BCE · Limestone · Sully Wing

Over 5,000 years old — from the very dawn of writing. A serpent (the king's name) stands above the serekh, the palace façade, topped by Horus the falcon. This is what Egyptian writing looked like before it became hieroglyphs.
