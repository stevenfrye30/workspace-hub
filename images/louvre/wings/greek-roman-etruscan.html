Winged Victory of Samothrace

c. 200–190 BCE · Parian marble · Daru Staircase

She stands at the top of the grand Daru staircase, wings flung back, drapery soaked and clinging as if she has just alighted on the prow of a ship. The ship itself is carved too — she came mounted on a stone warship in a pool of water on the sanctuary of Samothrace.

Discovered in 1863 in more than 100 fragments. Her head and arms have never been found.

Look from the right: the sculpture was designed to be seen from that side first — the drapery is more finished there.

Venus de Milo

c. 150–125 BCE · Parian marble · Sully Wing

Found in 1820 by a peasant on the Aegean island of Milos. France acquired her quickly — and when Louis XVIII presented her to the Louvre, the fact that her arms were missing was treated as accidental rather than a flaw.

She was almost certainly holding an apple, referencing the Judgment of Paris. Her missing arms let every viewer imagine her gesture.

Borghese Gladiator

Agasias of Ephesus
c. 100 BCE · Marble · Sully Wing

Not actually a gladiator — a warrior fighting a mounted opponent, arm raised with a (lost) shield. A tour de force of anatomical study; every muscle is taut with the effort of the moment before impact.

Sleeping Hermaphroditus

Roman copy of a Greek original · 2nd century CE · Sully Wing

From one angle, a sleeping woman. Walk around to the other side: the body is both. The mattress beneath — realistic enough that you want to touch it — was carved by Bernini in 1619 when the statue was restored.

Sarcophagus of the Spouses

c. 520 BCE · Etruscan terracotta · Denon Wing

A husband and wife recline together on a banquet couch for eternity. Painted terracotta, made in four pieces. The Etruscans, unlike the Greeks and Romans, gave women equal place at the feast — and in the tomb.
