P A I N T I N G S

13th – 19th century · Denon & Richelieu Wings

The Louvre's painting collection spans over 7,500 works. Here are a handful you should not miss.

Mona Lisa (La Joconde)

Leonardo da Vinci
c. 1503–1519 · Oil on poplar · Salle des États

Probably a portrait of Lisa Gherardini, wife of a Florentine merchant. Leonardo carried it with him for years, reworking it endlessly, and it never left his side until his death in France.

Her fame owes as much to theft as to genius: when Vincenzo Peruggia stole her in 1911 and kept her in a Paris apartment for two years, the empty wall drew larger crowds than the painting ever had.

Look closely: the landscape behind her left shoulder sits higher than the one behind her right — a subtle trick that makes her seem to shift as you move.

Liberty Leading the People

Eugène Delacroix
1830 · Oil on canvas · Denon Wing

Painted in the aftermath of the July Revolution that overthrew Charles X. Liberty — bare-breasted, tricolor raised, bayonet in hand — strides over the barricades and the dead.

The man in the top hat beside her is often read as a self-portrait. The boy with pistols would later inspire Gavroche in Hugo's Les Misérables.

The Raft of the Medusa

Théodore Géricault
1818–1819 · Oil on canvas · Denon Wing

A real and recent disaster: the French frigate Méduse ran aground off Senegal in 1816. The captain and officers took the lifeboats; 147 men were cut loose on a raft. Fifteen survived, after mutiny, thirst, and cannibalism.

Géricault interviewed survivors, visited morgues to study dead flesh, and built a scale model of the raft in his studio. The scandal reached the throne — the captain had been appointed through political favor.

The Coronation of Napoleon

Jacques-Louis David
1805–1807 · Oil on canvas · Denon Wing

Nearly 10 meters wide. Napoleon, having just placed the crown on his own head, is shown about to crown Joséphine. The Pope, summoned from Rome, sits behind — reduced to witness.

David inserted Napoleon's mother into the grandstand though she refused to attend. Painters, like emperors, revise history.

The Wedding at Cana

Paolo Veronese
1563 · Oil on canvas · Salle des États

The largest painting in the Louvre, at nearly 70 square meters. Commissioned for the refectory of a Benedictine monastery in Venice. Napoleon's troops took it in 1797 and cut it in half to transport it.

It hangs directly across from the Mona Lisa — and is almost entirely ignored by the crowds facing the other way.

The Lacemaker

Johannes Vermeer
c. 1669–1670 · Oil on canvas on panel · Richelieu Wing

One of Vermeer's smallest works — only about 24 centimeters tall. A young woman bends over her bobbins with the intense near-vision of close handwork.

Salvador Dalí obsessed over it. He was once allowed to set up an easel in the Louvre to copy it, and produced a small canvas covered in rhinoceros horns instead.

La Grande Odalisque

Jean-Auguste-Dominique Ingres
1814 · Oil on canvas · Denon Wing

Commissioned by Napoleon's sister, Caroline Murat, Queen of Naples. Ingres gave his odalisque two or three extra vertebrae — anatomically impossible, visually ideal. Critics howled; the distortion is now the point.

The Cheat with the Ace of Diamonds

Georges de La Tour
c. 1636–1638 · Oil on canvas · Sully Wing

Four figures, four glances, one con. A naïve young nobleman is about to lose his fortune to the cheat on the left, who has pulled an ace from his belt. The courtesan and her servant are in on it; only the young man isn't looking at anyone.

A moral painting: the three traditional sins of youth — gambling, wine, and women — all at the same table.
